---
title: "Noyce, Lederman, Evans 2018: Construction of an infectious horsepox virus"
source: "PLOS ONE, 2018"
---

Noyce et al. reconstructed horsepox virus, an extinct relative of smallpox (variola), from synthetic DNA fragments. The work was conducted at the University of Alberta and published in PLOS ONE. The protocol involved ordering approximately 240 kilobases of synthetic DNA as overlapping fragments from a commercial synthesis provider, cotransfecting the fragments into cells infected with a fowlpox helper virus, and recovering recombinant horsepox via plaque purification. The reconstruction demonstrated that a 200+ kb DNA virus genome could be assembled entirely from synthetic fragments available through commercial providers, without biological horsepox material. The paper generated significant controversy because horsepox DNA was not regulated (variola is a CDC/WHO-controlled select agent, but horsepox was not), demonstrating a regulatory gap. The paper is cited as evidence that synthesis screening must apply to sequences with structural homology to controlled pathogens, not just exact matches. The protocols described match the select_agent_synthesis taxonomy category.
